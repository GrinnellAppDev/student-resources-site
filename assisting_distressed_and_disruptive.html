Disruptive Student

A student who is disruptive is interfering with the College's learning enviornment due to behavior that is disruptive or dangerous. Disruptive students frequently experience on-going or extreme levels of stress without the appropriate coping skills; this indicates potential escalation if not resolved.

Recognize

Potential disruptive student behaviors:

  • Inability to regulate emotion or heightened emotion
  • Verbal or physical threats to self or others
  • Agression or deviancy
  • Recklessness or destructiveness
  • Resistance to help

Respond & Refer
  • Remain calm. Safety first. The welfare of the student and the College community is our top priority.
  • Trust your instinct. Call the appropriate resource for help. When in doubt, call Campus Safety and Security x4600 or the Dean of Students Office x3714.
  • Remember: It is not your role to provide the professional help needed for a disruptive student. You only need to make the necessary call and request assistance.
  • Don't be afraid to directly ask the student if they have thoughts of harming themselves or others. If yes, do not leave the student. If necessary, find someone to stay with the student while you call the appropriate resources.


Distressed Student

A student who is distressed is typically experiening situational stressors and would benefit from your care and concern. It is important to refer or consult with additional resources to create a network to support a distressed student in overcoming their obstacles and devloping good self-advocacy. Distressed students may exhibit mildly disruptive behaviors.

Recognize

Potentional distressed student behaviors:

  • Very sad or irritable
  • Unduly anxious
  • Mood or behavior changes
  • Lacking motivation or concentration
  • Infequent attendance, avoidance, or withdrawn
  • Lethargy or frequently falling asleep in class
  • Marked changes to hygiene/appearance
  • References to self-harm or suicidal thoughts

Respond & Refer
  • Trust your instinct. If in doubt, reach out. Call to consult with a colleague or campus resource.
  • Address the situation on a more personal level in a private setting. Express concern and interest. Even if the problem doesn't seem urgent to you, it may be very important to the student.
  • If expectations aren't being met, deal directly with the behavior/problem according to your classroom protocol.
  • Determine the student's willingness to get help. Reassure the student that it is an act of strength to ask for help.
  • Refer the student to one of the College's resources. Offer to make the initial contact or walk them over.
  • Remember: reaching out doesn't mean you are responsible for the student's emotional wellbeing or that you must (or can) resolve the student's difficulties. Know your limits and refer to the appropriate resources.
  • Don't be afraid to directly ask the student if they have thoughts of harming themselves or others. If yes, do not leave the student. If necessary, find someone to stay with the student while you call the appropriate resources.


Privacy Laws and Confidentiality

The Family Education Right and Privacy Act (FERPA) permits communication about a student of concern in connection with health and safety emergency. Oberservations of a student's conduct or statements made my the student are not FERPA protected. Such information should be thoughtfully shared out of respect for the student's privacy.
